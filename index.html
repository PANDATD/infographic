The Unfulfilled Promise

A Data-Driven Assessment of Tribal Welfare Under Kerala's Communist Government

The Paradox of the "Kerala Model"

Kerala is celebrated for its high human development indicators, a model of progress for the nation. However, this success masks a stark reality: the state's tribal (Adivasi) communities have been systematically excluded from these advancements, revealing a deep chasm between the government's pro-tribal rhetoric and the on-ground reality.

A People Left Behind: The Socio-Economic Gap

Endemic Poverty

An overwhelming majority of Kerala's tribal population lives in poverty, a stark contrast to the general population.

Widespread Landlessness

The historical 1963 Land Reform Act inadvertently led to Adivasi land alienation. Today, most are landless wage laborers.

Deep Educational Disparities

While the state average is high, literacy among specific tribal groups like the Kattunaikan and Muthuvan is alarmingly low.

Anatomy of a Failure: The Vidyavahini Scheme

A critical scheme to transport tribal students in Wayanad from remote hamlets to schools stalled, exemplifying systemic failure. The issue wasn't a lack of policy, but a simple failure in financial execution by the Tribal Department, leaving hundreds of children stranded.

1. Unstable Administration

Responsibility for the scheme shifted between departments multiple times, diffusing accountability.

2. Funding Failure

The Tribal Department, responsible for funding, defaulted on payments to taxi operators for months.

3. Service Withdrawal

With significant arrears pending, drivers refused to operate, halting the transport service.

4. Dire Consequences

Students unable to reach school, leading to a high risk of dropouts and jeopardizing their future. As of June 2025, over 200 schools were affected.

The Perpetual Struggle for Land

Land rights remain the most critical and unresolved issue for Adivasis. Government actions often amount to "landfare"â€”substituting genuine land reform with piecemeal projects and symbolic gestures that fail to provide substantive ownership or sustainable livelihoods.

Decades of Waiting

The path to land rights is fraught with extreme delays. The case of 127 families in Nelliyampathy, who received land titles but still cannot legally pay tax on their land after 6 years, highlights a system of symbolic gestures over real rights.

Funding Failures for Resettlement

Even funds allocated for tribal resettlement are chronically underspent, indicating a lack of urgency and capacity in implementing land programs. The TRDM scheme is a prime example.

Following the Money: A Leaky Pipe

Despite declared budgets, tribal welfare appears to be a low priority during fiscal stress. Funds are cut, diverted, or simply left unspent, crippling essential services for the most vulnerable communities.

Schemes on the Chopping Block

In a recent year, citing a financial crisis, the government slashed or completely cut funding for 27 out of 49 tribal welfare schemes, impacting 1.5 lakh families.
